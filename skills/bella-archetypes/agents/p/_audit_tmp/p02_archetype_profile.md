# The Supported Participant

## Summary

A self-voicing adult with disability who is willing and engaged but relies on trusted intermediaries — a good support coordinator, a partner, an informal advocate — for navigation, comprehension, and emotional reassurance. Overwhelmed by NDIS jargon, clinical reports, and administrative processes. Experiences anxiety about making mistakes that could cost them funding. Wants tools that translate the system into human language, reduce paperwork panic, and make them feel like a person rather than a number.

## Behavioural Profile

- **SC-dependent satisfaction.** Both Kylie and Melanie describe their current SC (Amanda) as transformative. Previous SC experiences were poor. Satisfaction pivots entirely on the quality of this single relationship.
- **Jargon as barrier.** Kylie: wants "easy to read simple English." Melanie: "the language" is the one thing she'd fix. Both rate reading clinical reports 5/5 difficulty.
- **Anxiety-driven engagement.** Kylie: ADHD makes form-filling terrifying ("what if I get it wrong and it costs me?"). Melanie: initial plan receipt caused "panic" because she didn't know what to do.
- **Informal support networks.** Kylie's husband double-checks paperwork. Melanie's husband accompanies appointments. Both rely on Amanda for guidance beyond formal SC scope.
- **Cognitive accessibility needs.** Kylie: auditory/verbal processing preference, needs colour coding and bullet points, cannot retain information not used daily. Melanie: "Swiss cheese brain" from neuropathy, cognitive fog.

## Motivations & Goals

**Functional:** Understand what their plan actually means | Find trustworthy providers without gambling | Complete paperwork without anxiety | Have their story recorded once and shared, not retold
**Emotional:** Feel like a person, not a number | Feel confident they won't accidentally lose funding | Feel that someone genuinely cares about their situation | Feel that the system is on their side

## Pain Points

1. **[Plan comprehension — 5/5]** --> Melanie: "10 out of 5." Kylie: "50/50 understanding." Plans are impenetrable documents written in clinical language.
2. **[Provider discovery — 5/5]** --> Kylie: "the Wild West." Melanie: "panicked" at receiving plan with no guidance on how to use it.
3. **[Retelling trauma]** --> Having to explain disability story to every new provider. Kylie suggested a centralised evidence vault. Melanie: "even when a referral was available."
4. **[ADHD/cognitive barriers]** --> Kylie: can't retain jargon, needs visual/audio learning. Melanie: cognitive fog, forgets what she's read.
5. **[Being a number]** --> Kylie: "I don't want to be a number." Melanie: "It feels like they're giving you funding but don't want to teach you how to use it."

## Brand & Positioning Implications

| Dimension | Implication |
|---|---|
| **Messaging** | Lead with warmth, clarity, and simplification. "Your plan, in plain English." Acknowledge the system is confusing — it's not their fault. |
| **Pricing** | Plan-funded is ideal — they may not have personal budget for tools. Must be justified through SC or plan. |
| **Features** | Easy Read, Evidence Vault (centralised story), plain-language plan translation, visual budget tracking, provider recommendations. |
| **Red flags** | Jargon in the product itself, clinical/deficit framing, complex onboarding, assumption of system literacy, any language that makes them feel stupid. |
| **Accessibility** | ADHD: audio/video content, colour coding, bullet points, short text. Cognitive fog: progressive disclosure, reminders, simple navigation. |
| **Trust** | Build through SC recommendation. If Amanda says it's good, they'll try it. Peer endorsement > marketing. |

## Confidence: Medium — Kylie (2 sessions, well-observed) provides solid anchor. Melanie (1 session) adds breadth but lower depth. The archetype likely represents a large portion of the NDIS population — the "silent majority" who are overwhelmed but don't have the professional skills of the Empowered Navigator.
