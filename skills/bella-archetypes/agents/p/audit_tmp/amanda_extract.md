# Amanda Riches — Behavioural Profile Extract

## 1. Professional Profile

**Role:** Support Coordinator — complex level (Level 3), operating as sole trader and also contracting through a larger SC company.

**Tenure:** Active since the second or third year of the NDIS rollout (approximately 2015-2016), making her one of the earliest practitioners in the scheme. Over a decade of continuous SC experience.

**Caseload:** Currently approximately 15 participants; half classified as complex. Previously held caseloads of nearly 40, which she describes as unsustainable ("I did not cope under any circumstances"). Evolved deliberately toward fewer, more complex cases over time.

**Specialisation:** National support coordination at complex level. Works across multiple jurisdictions (NSW, Victoria, Queensland) and across systems — NDIS, TAC (Transport Accident Commission, Victoria), My Aged Care. Works with degenerative disabilities, acquired brain injury, high-care medical cases (tracheostomy, ventilation), hospital interface and transitions.

**Background before SC:** Not explicitly stated in transcripts, though she references learning legislative and clinical frameworks from the ground up rather than from formal training ("I'm not clinically trained"; "I've only learnt parts of the legislation because I had to"). Her mother is an NDIS participant, which gives her dual lived-experience perspective.

**Business model:** Operates as a sole trader but also works under a company. References "my boss for the company that I work for" and also speaks about paying for software "as a business owner ... for myself and my team." Appears to hold a hybrid independent/contracted arrangement.

**Location:** Rural/remote area near Penrith, NSW. This is a defining feature of her practice — rural service delivery shapes cost structures, provider availability, and travel calculations.

## 2. Observable Behaviours & Decision Patterns

### Decision-making approach
- **Evidence-first, legislation-anchored.** Builds arguments from legislation upward: "I also had to figure out what breaches of legislation that they did in that report. I also had to figure out, they actually breached one of his UN rights." (S1)
- **Budget-first on new plans.** Immediately calculates budget line-by-line on receiving any new plan, anticipating shortfalls before they become crises.
- **Quality gatekeeping with providers.** Every allied health report is reviewed and returned if insufficient: "the amount I push reports back is every single report" (S2). She starts the end-of-plan process three months out specifically because of report back-and-forth cycles.
- **Confrontational when warranted.** Pushes back against the NDIA, plan managers, and providers: "they do try and push back on me and I push back just as hard. I dig my heels in quite often" (S1). Also pushes back on providers: "If they go, nah, we're not doing it, then I go, well, are you the right person for the job?" (S2)

### Handling ambiguity
- Consults peer network (former Level 6 planner, senior coordinators) to gut-check decisions: "I will often call them and say, I need some insight. Am I missing something?" (S1)
- Accepts the fundamentally unpredictable nature of the NDIS: "nothing has ever changed with the NDIS" and "you think you've cracked the code and then you put in something almost identical and it gets rejected" (S1)
- Default approach is pragmatic patience: "Let's just see where it goes. Let's just sit back" (S1)

### Prioritisation
- Participant safety is the absolute priority, overriding administrative compliance: "I'm not going to use funding on a stupid report for a client who needs support" (S1)
- Front-loads effort in rural contexts (60% at plan start) to secure providers and verify rates, rather than the standard 40%.
- Will sacrifice her own SC report to preserve participant funding: "If they come back and argue it with me, I'll tell them, well, give me more funding then" (S1)

### Collaboration patterns
- Care team meetings initiated three months before plan end to align all clinicians.
- Reports collected sequentially: all non-OT reports first, then consolidated by OT.
- Client communication via structured post-action emails that double as file notes and checklists.
- Strongly values long-term provider relationships (GEMS since 2019) over transactional ones.

## 3. Functional Goals

1. **Secure adequate NDIS plan funding** to cover real-world costs including rural travel, SACS award rates, and crisis contingencies.
2. **Build participant capacity** toward self-management of their NDIS, ultimately aiming to "put myself out of the job if I can" (S1).
3. **Ensure report completeness and legislative alignment** before submission to reduce rejection risk and repeat plan reviews.
4. **Coordinate multi-disciplinary care teams** across jurisdictions to produce coherent, evidence-based submissions.
5. **Vet and maintain a reliable provider network** in rural areas where options are scarce and quality inconsistent.
6. **Reduce unbillable administrative work** to bring actual hours closer to funded hours.
7. **Achieve transparency with participants** about work being done on their behalf — "they can see what I'm working on" (S4/S5).

## 5. Pain Points & Challenges

1. **[Unbillable work — 10/10]** → Funding shortfalls force her to perform ~20% of total work unpaid → She cannot stop working because participant safety depends on it; erodes financial sustainability.

2. **[Insufficient plan funding — systemic]** → Every single current plan is underfunded; trend worsening over 6-9 months → Crisis situations where participants run out of money mid-plan, risk hospitalisation or death.

3. **[Three-month funding instalments — "sleepless nights"]** → Designed to prevent fraud but catastrophically mismatched to complex/degenerative cases → Quarterly funding drops from $6,819 to $2,083 to $1,874; plan reviews take minimum 3 months, during which participants may deteriorate fatally.

4. **[Non-compliant or fraudulent providers — high end of spectrum]** → Limited rural provider pool increases risk of bad referrals → Participant must retell their story; SC must find replacement provider in thin market; emotional harm to participant.

5. **[NDIA rejection without rationale]** → Comprehensive 200-page reports rejected as "not reasonable and necessary" without explanation → Demoralising; wastes months of multi-professional effort; no clear path to improvement.

6. **[Value for money pushback — increasing]** → NDIA using "value for money" to reject OT/physio recommendations even with supporting evidence → She counters with cost-benefit analysis and peer-reviewed citations, but the burden is escalating.

7. **[Billing codes and invoicing support — 6-7/10, daily]** → Must assist providers and participants unfamiliar with NDIS billing → Time-consuming and unrewarded.

8. **[Rural provider scarcity]** → Over 50% of allied health budgets consumed by travel alone → Telehealth unsuitable for many complex/degenerative clients.

9. **[Report quality variance]** → Many allied health providers produce inadequate reports → She must return reports repeatedly; some providers resist, creating conflict.

10. **[Handover quality from other SCs]** → Received a 1.5-page handover for a complex participant that cost $500 → Had to rebuild entire picture from scratch.

## 9. Value System & Professional Philosophy

**Core identity:** Service-driven advocate. "I am a very sort of service-driven person" (S1). Being unable to serve participants due to high caseloads was experienced as a personal affront: "I haunted that. I absolutely hated it because that's not who I am as a person."

**Defining trade-off:** Will sacrifice her own financial interests (unbilled work, skipped SC reports) to protect participant funding and safety. Will not sacrifice participant safety for administrative compliance.

**What "good SC work" means to her:**
- Deep relationships over transactional service
- Face-to-face home visits to truly understand participant needs
- Building participant capacity toward independence where possible
- Accepting that some participants will always need support (degenerative disabilities) — rejecting the "lazy participants" framing
- Reports that meet legislative standards AND reflect real-world lived experience
- Holding the entire care team to account, including providers, NDIA planners, and plan managers
- Transparency with participants about all actions taken on their behalf

**Professional evolution:** Has moved from volume (40 participants) to depth (15 complex). This was a deliberate values-driven shift, not circumstantial. Senior SCs gravitate to complex work because "they now know the system, we know the workings of the system a bit better."

**Peer network reliance:** Regularly consults former NDIA insiders and senior SC colleagues. Recommended two of them for the co-design programme. This network is how she manages ambiguity in a system without clear rules.

**Accountability stance:** Holds both herself and others accountable. References emailing clients after every action as both a transparency measure and self-check: "that's part of my daily practice now" (S1).

## 10. Technology & Tool Evaluation

**Current tools:** Calendar, pen and paper, email, Google Drive, plan manager portals, ChatGPT (with manual de-identification).

**Attitude to technology:** Pragmatic daily user, not an early adopter by disposition. Uses AI as a practical utility (legislation lookup, email drafting) rather than as an exploration tool. Strict about privacy: manually strips all identifying information before using ChatGPT.

**What drives adoption decisions:**
- **Time saving must be demonstrable.** Values the tool at "$50 to $100 for complex cases... because that's $100 an hour of my time that I'm not using" (S4/S5). The conversion must be "several hours saved for about an hour of my time."
- **Consistency and ease of use.** "The tool needs consistency, to be as easy and quick as possible, and to require minimal administrative effort, particularly on the participant side" (S4/S5).
- **Visual and fast.** Drawn to colour-coded quality grading, one-look overview interfaces: "Just colours, visual, very quick" (S2).
- **Privacy and security are non-negotiable.** "My biggest fear with AI... is the risk of intense participant information being exposed to hackers" (S1).
- **Must not create duplicate work.** Acknowledged plan managers already track spend; doesn't want to replicate that in BellaAssist unless there's clear additive value.

**What drives recommendation decisions:**
- Would recommend BellaAssist to colleagues "100%" (S4/S5).
- Would onboard "all of them" — 100% of caseload — if available for free; only exception would be if a participant declined.
- As a business owner, prefers paying for software directly over billing to participant plans because business purchases are simpler and avoid NDIS justification burden.

**Key feature reactions:**
- Document Intelligence: "invaluable" (S2)
- Handover Pack: "I absolutely love this" (S2)
- Submission Readiness Report: strong validation, particularly on legislative compliance checking
- Bella AI Assistant: wanted it to function as a searchable document engine with citations; frustrated when it couldn't read document content (S4/S5)
- My Voice feature: "both are good depending on what you want to achieve" — valued the detailed version citing multiple sources (S4/S5)
- Provider portal concept: suggested providers should have their own access to upload and receive feedback directly, cutting SC out of basic feedback loop (S2)

**Accessibility advocacy:** Repeatedly raised eye gaze and voice-to-text integration as essential for participants with high spinal cord injuries and profound disabilities. This was her first unsolicited suggestion in S2.

## 12. Brand & Positioning Sensitivity

**Language and framing that resonates:**
- Practical, time-saving value propositions grounded in hours saved
- Legislative compliance as a selling point — "compared to legislation" gives confidence to push back on NDIA and to challenge providers
- Participant-centred ownership — data belongs to the person, tool follows the participant
- Transparency language — "keeping everything transparent and above board" (S4/S5)
- Empowerment framing for both SCs and participants

**What she values in professional tools:**
- Speed and visual clarity over feature density
- Actionable outputs (checklists, draft emails) not just reports
- Ability to share/forward specific feedback to providers
- Paint-by-numbers simplicity for participants with option for advanced SC view
- Tool should serve new SCs as guidance AND experienced SCs as efficiency

**Red flags in marketing:**
- Any suggestion of replacing support coordinators or diminishing the human element would be rejected: "We're never going to be out of a job completely" (S2)
- Over-promising on AI capabilities when privacy is not ironclad
- Framing that implies participants are "lazy" or should be capacity-building off the scheme
- Products that create more administrative burden than they save
- Anything that feels like it was built without understanding complex/degenerative cases: she explicitly calls out that solutions built for stable spinal cord injury cases do not translate to degenerative conditions

**Pricing sensitivity:**
- $50-$100 per year per complex participant from personal business budget
- $499/plan cycle to participant plan is acceptable IF under low-cost AT threshold ($500)
- Strongly prefers SC business purchase model over participant plan billing — less friction, avoids NDIA justification
- If on approved supports list: "Easy. Not a problem then" (S4/S5)
