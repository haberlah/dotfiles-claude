# The Growth Pragmatist

## Summary

A business-owner SC who optimises relentlessly for operational efficiency and practice growth. Views NDIS support coordination as an enterprise to build, not just a calling to follow. Evaluates every tool, process, and relationship through a return-on-investment lens while maintaining genuine commitment to participant outcomes. Adapts to system changes rather than fighting them — pragmatic, not cynical.

---

## Behavioural Profile

### Decision-making
- **ROI-anchored.** Every decision runs through a time-cost calculation. Bailey: "The more efficient I am, the less money. So it's a very funny game." Craig: "Should I earn less cuz I'm efficient?"
- **Wait-and-see on regulation.** Neither panics about NDIS reforms. Bailey refused to spend $15K on premature registration. Craig monitors but doesn't pre-empt.
- **Evidence-heavy submissions.** Both front-load evidence and submit early. Bailey requests "a million pieces of evidence just to cover that basis." Craig submits concise packages (~6 documents) from elite providers.

### Workflow patterns
- **Structured weekly cadence.** Bailey: Monday portal reviews, dedicated task-type days. Craig: Monthly billing cycle, geographic batching of client visits.
- **Systems over staff.** Bailey deliberately avoids hiring ("I don't actually think anyone else will care as much"). Craig uses contractors to avoid employment overheads.
- **Delegation by design.** Craig routes non-complex referrals to team members. Bailey outsources administrative functions to technology.

### Risk management
- **Provider vetting as investment.** Both invest heavily upfront in provider quality to avoid rework. Bailey meets directors personally. Craig refers almost exclusively to one elite OT firm (Verve).
- **Risk framing in submissions.** Both frame submissions around worst-day scenarios and consequences of funding withdrawal, not positive outcomes.

### Technology stance
- **AI as operational amplifier.** Bailey uses AI as a "virtual colleague" — email drafting, evidence compilation, report review. Craig sees AI value in copy-paste-ready output and perception enhancement, not workflow replacement.
- **Strict privacy hygiene.** Bailey de-identifies everything before AI use ("I always put John Doe"). Craig's evaluation centres on output quality, not process novelty.
- **Burden test dominant.** Craig's primary evaluation criterion: "Don't create more work." If the tool surfaces 35 errors to triage, he disengages.

## Motivations & Goals

### Functional Goals
- **Scale** caseload ethically without proportional increase in unbillable work
- **Maintain** business independence and avoid returning to employment
- **Streamline** administrative processes to convert saved time into business development or client-facing hours
- **Build** a defensible evidence package with minimal rework cycles
- **Position** the business for future NDIS landscape changes (navigation contracts, registration shifts)

### Emotional Goals
- **Feel** that efficiency and expertise are rewarded rather than penalised by the billing model
- **Feel** autonomous and in control of business direction despite regulatory uncertainty
- **Feel** validated that their pragmatic, tech-forward approach is the right way to operate
- **Feel** confident that recommended providers will deliver — avoiding reputational damage
- **Feel** connected despite working alone (Bailey) or managing remote operations (Craig)

## Pain Points & Challenges

1. **[Efficiency paradox under billable hours]** --> Being more efficient = less revenue --> Accept the trade-off on principle but recognise the structural perverse incentive. Pain: systemic, existential.

2. **[Unbillable admin and phone time]** --> NDIS hold times, onboarding work, free services for underfunded participants --> Cannot bill for essential coordination work. Bailey: 5/5 pain, daily.

3. **[NDIS processing delays]** --> Change-of-situation requests taking 4 months instead of 21 days --> Work is done but participants and the business are stuck waiting. Bailey: "magic wand" fix.

4. **[Tool overhead vs value]** --> New tools must demonstrably save time, not add admin --> Craig: "Whatever comes of it, it kind of just needs to make sure it doesn't create more work."

5. **[Regulatory uncertainty]** --> Future of SC role unclear (navigator model, registration changes) --> Both manage anxiety through wait-and-see pragmatism, but it's a background stressor.

6. **[Handover quality from other SCs]** --> Participants arrive with no documentation --> Must rebuild from scratch. Bailey: "I've received 10 participants with no handover."

## Context

- **Work setting:** Home-based (Bailey in Adelaide; Craig in Beacon Hill/Jindabyne, sometimes overseas). Sole operator or small team. No institutional support structure.
- **Time pressures:** Caseload growth (Bailey: 19 to 25 in weeks). Plan review prep starts 2-3 months ahead.
- **Regulatory:** Operating during NDIS transition. Both unregistered or strategically registered. Monitoring reforms without reacting prematurely.
- **Technology:** Both use CRM (Astalty/SPLOSE), accounting software, email. Bailey adds AI tools (ChatGPT). Craig operates a more traditional stack.
- **Financial model:** Revenue directly tied to billable hours. Every unbillable minute is a direct cost. Per-user tool pricing preferred over participant-plan billing.

## Brand & Positioning Implications

| Dimension | Implication |
|---|---|
| **Messaging tone** | Direct, business-focused, no-nonsense. Avoid emotional appeals. Lead with efficiency metrics and time savings. |
| **Value proposition** | Frame as a business tool that pays for itself: "X hours saved per plan review." Must demonstrate ROI within the first interaction. |
| **Pricing** | Per-user monthly subscription ($40-50/month range). Against participant-plan billing. Enterprise/company billing preferred. |
| **Feature emphasis** | Document intelligence, evidence compilation, copy-paste-ready output. NOT guidance/hand-holding features. |
| **Onboarding** | Fast, self-serve, minimal setup. Demo must show immediate value. Craig: "Quick wins." |
| **Red flags** | Feature overload, false-positive noise, participant-facing tool emphasis, emotional language, slow interfaces, high error/triage burden. |
| **Channel** | LinkedIn, SC peer networks, sole-trader business communities, direct outreach to growing practices. |
| **Trust building** | Show output quality, not process novelty. Craig evaluates by reading the generated text, not by understanding the AI methodology. |
