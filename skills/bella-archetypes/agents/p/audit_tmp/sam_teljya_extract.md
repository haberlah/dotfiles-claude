# Sam & Teljya Steele — Behavioural Profile Extract

## 1. Personal Profile & NDIS Context

### Who Is Who

**Sam Steele** — Father/carer. Not the NDIS participant. Works in corporate communications and also in the AI/technology space (email domains: sam@ainative.net.au, sam@bluesteeleconsulting.com). Handled the NDIS application process, manages budget tracking, and conducts the bulk of research into Zariya's support needs. Describes himself as advantaged and literate in navigating bureaucratic systems.

**Teljya Steele** — Mother/carer. Not the NDIS participant. Works in corporate communications ("I write for a living... I'm forever aligning people's language to some narrative"). Manages day-to-day provider coordination and budget allocation. Uses employer-provided PC/Teams setup but personally uses a Mac. The more emotionally articulate of the pair; more willing to express frustration and more attuned to language/branding nuances.

**Zariya Steele** — The NDIS participant. Aged 6.5 at time of interviews. Diagnosed with Autism Level 2 and ADHD (comorbid, diagnosed approximately December 2025/January 2026). Currently in the Early Childhood Early Intervention stream. Likes therapy (play-based) and one-on-one adult attention. All therapy is school-based; no home-based services.

### Family Background
- Returned to Australia from Singapore in early 2025. The children had spent their entire lives overseas.
- Zariya and her sister enrolled in school on the Central Coast, NSW.
- The environmental change triggered behavioural issues in Zariya, prompting investigation.
- The family is highly educated, dual-income, English-speaking — and explicitly aware this puts them at an advantage relative to most NDIS families.

### NDIS History
- **Diagnosis path:** Teljya researched the evaluation pathway. Bypassed the 6-12 month paediatrician waitlist by going directly to a clinical psychologist, who provided the official Autism Level 2 diagnosis and flagged NDIS eligibility.
- **Application:** Sam lodged the NDIS application in the first week of May 2025. Told to expect up to 12 weeks processing.
- **Delay:** In July 2025, while the family was driving in New Zealand, NDIS called about incomplete paperwork (a missing signature). Teljya signed the document from Sam's phone while he drove. Then told they were "now in the queue."
- **Approval:** Approximately one month later. A phone call to confirm details, then a one-hour planning interview with a planner, followed by unilateral delivery of the NDIS plan as a PDF via email.
- **Plan approved:** 27 October 2025.
- **Plan management:** Chose plan-managed (provider choice retained, plan manager handles billing). Plan manager selected based on recommendation from an allied health group.
- **Plan structure:** Simple — two line items only: (1) Plan management fee, (2) Improved Daily Living Skills (capacity building). No support coordination funding included.
- **No support coordinator.** Sam confused the Local Area Coordinator (LAC) in Gosford with a support coordinator. Once clarified, confirmed they have no SC and had essentially forgotten the LAC existed.
- **No functional capacity assessment** completed. Were advised it might be needed for plan review but have not yet done one.
- **ADHD diagnosis added** approximately December 2025/January 2026, prompted by the OT's in-classroom observations. Sam initially dismissed the possibility due to his own limited knowledge of ADHD.

## 11. Brand & Marketing Sensitivity

### What Feels Respectful (Both)
- Tools that acknowledge the intelligence and agency of the user — neither parent wants to be talked down to.
- Practical, outcome-focused communication. They respond to demonstrations of real capability, not aspirational marketing.
- Acknowledging that the NDIS is complex even for educated, capable people — normalising the struggle.

### What Feels Patronising or Problematic
- **"Evidence Vault"** — Teljya found this "arresting" and "very criminally." She flagged it as potentially insensitive when applied to children and caregiving. Recommends nomenclature testing with users.
- Any implication that parents should already know how to navigate the system.
- Language that frames disability support in deficit terms rather than functional and strengths-based terms (Teljya would notice this immediately given her communications background).

### What Resonates
- **"NDIS lawyer in your pocket"** — Teljya's spontaneous description of BellaAssist. This is advocacy, protection, and expertise — not charity or hand-holding.
- Showing concrete capability (S2 demo generated genuine excitement: "it's exciting, so I hope we can play around with the real thing one day").
- AI-powered features are appealing to both — they are already AI users and see AI as a natural solution.

### Pricing/Value Sensitivity
- Not directly discussed. The family is dual-income, corporate-professional, and was already paying privately for therapies before NDIS. Price sensitivity is likely lower than average. Value proposition would need to be framed around time savings and optimisation rather than affordability.
