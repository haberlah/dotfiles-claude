History of suicidality, frequent hospital admissions. Currently doing well on ketamine, described as a "game-changer," though Wendy notes Tess's pattern of metabolising medications and developing tolerance.

- **NDIS history:** Approximately 3+ years on the NDIS. Accessed via NEMA (mental health access pathway). Brotherhood of St Laurence served as Local Area Coordinator. First year described as a "complete waste of funds" due to unskilled providers. Things improved dramatically after finding Jane.
- **Plan type:** Plan-managed (via My Plan Manager, recommended by Jane). Self-management was impossible during Tess's acute crisis period — "It was a real survival time."
- **Family structure:** Tess previously married (wife), lived in Hampton (southern Melbourne). Now back home with Wendy and family in Ascot Vale (western suburbs). Husband present during interview (doing drilling in background). A "Michael" is briefly mentioned (likely a family member — asks to let dad in).
- **Wendy's professional background:** Retired school principal. Previously worked for McKillop Family Services with families of children with disabilities. Strong background in bureaucracy, funding, education leadership, and meeting management.
- **Support team ("Team Tess"):** Jane (psychosocial recovery coach), My Plan Manager (plan manager), OT specialising in mental health, support worker Sammy, GP, psychiatrist, neuropsychiatrist. Wendy uses the "Team Tess" concept extensively.
- **Advocacy involvement:** Registered with Tandem (mental health carer advocacy organisation in Victoria). Puts her hand up for consultation opportunities when they arise. Motivated by systemic change.

## 5. Pain Points & Frustrations

### Frustration 1: Evidence submitted but not read
- **Trigger:** Thousands spent on reports and assessments for plan review; NDIS did not read the submissions. Personal letter to NDIS about how disability affects life was also disregarded.
- **Consequence:** Felt disrespected. "By not reading, you're disrespecting it." Resulted in a rollover of the plan after all effort spent — "I just wish we'd known that at the start."
- **Workaround:** None available. Continues to submit comprehensive evidence because "you never know when they will or won't" read it.

### Frustration 2: The deficit model / retraumatising review process
- **Trigger:** Plan review requires highlighting sickness and neediness. "You're almost saying, 'I'm so sick. I'm so needy. I'm so bad, but you've got to give me money.'"
- **Consequence:** Emotional toll on family. Feels like "begging for something that is absolutely genuinely needed." Described review process in one word: "Hell."
- **Workaround:** Team Tess approach distributes the emotional burden across trusted providers.

### Frustration 3: Functional assessment irrelevant for mental health
- **Trigger:** OTs conducting functional capacity assessments using physical/ADL frameworks that do not capture mental health complexity. Young OTs "didn't know what to ask."
- **Consequence:** Wasted money and time. Earlier assessments were "more triggering for Tess than not." Required changing OTs multiple times.
- **Workaround:** Found (through Jane) an OT who specialises in mental health — "she's actually brilliant."

### Frustration 4: Unskilled providers in first year
- **Trigger:** Three support coordinators/recovery coaches failed — one warranted a formal complaint, one was a well-meaning 22-year-old without skills, one forgettable. "Complete waste of funds."
- **Consequence:** Wendy in tears calling LAC. Money spent without supporting Tess. "We weren't supporting Tess. It was just ridiculous."
- **Workaround:** LAC recommended Jane. Jane was "a game-changer."

### Frustration 5: Repetitive storytelling without benefit
- **Trigger:** Having to tell Tess's complex story to each new provider. Acceptable when information is used "sensitively and appropriately" — unacceptable when providers lack skills to understand it or disregard it.
- **Consequence:** Retraumatising for the family. Wendy immediately resonated with BellaAssist's promise of evidence in one place: "that in itself is really retraumatizing."

### Frustration 6: "Gotcha" review process
- **Trigger:** Plan review meeting with only hours' notice, no support person allowed in the room, adversarial rather than collaborative tone.
- **Consequence:** Felt like "a gotcha moment rather than collaborative." Wendy identifies this as systemically dangerous for people without advocates, with English as a second language, or with intellectual disability.
- **Workaround:** Wendy's professional experience allowed her to manage — but she explicitly flags that most people could not.

### Frustration 7: 48-week funding for a 52-week disability
- **Trigger:** LAC informed them funding covers 48 weeks. "Tess's disability doesn't go those four weeks."
- **Consequence:** Absurd and frustrating. Wendy uses dark humour: responded to interviewer's "depression holiday" quip with "Let's do that, shall we? All of us."

### Frustration 8: LAC quality lottery
- **Trigger:** Current LAC described as unhelpful — "all about well I don't do that and I don't do that." Previous LAC was "amazing" and "would go to bat for you."
- **Consequence:** Inconsistent support quality. Feels like "a postcode lottery... it's the person."
- **Workaround:** Works around poor LAC by relying on Team Tess.

**Confidence: OBSERVED (S1) — frustrations were the primary focus of DITL and are richly documented**

## 11. Brand & Marketing Sensitivity

### Language sensitivity
- **Reacts negatively to:** Deficit framing, bureaucratic language that dehumanises, tokenistic phrases like "have an ordinary life" ("I'm glad I missed that one"), "choice and control" when used as rhetoric rather than practice.
- **Responds positively to:** Empathy, integrity, honesty, vulnerability, "genuine engagement," evidence of care. These are the words she used to describe Jane — the gold standard in her experience.
- **Balances critique with fairness:** Will always acknowledge the good before criticising. Any marketing that only attacks the NDIS without acknowledging its value would likely feel inauthentic to her.

### Trust signals
- **Peer recommendation is paramount.** Found NEMA through research. Found Jane through LAC recommendation. Uses My Plan Manager on Jane's recommendation. Joined this trial through Jane's introduction.
- **Demonstrated competence over credentials.** Values OTs who understand mental health over OTs with qualifications but no mental health skill. Values what people can do, not their title.
- **Trustworthiness must be earned.** "They have demonstrated their trustworthiness." Not given freely — earned through action over time.

### Potential sensitivities for BellaAssist positioning
- **Will reject tools that feel like more admin for admin's sake.** Already exhausted by "ticker box" requirements. A tool must demonstrably reduce burden, not add to it.
- **Not personally emotionally motivated to use a storytelling tool.** "Not because I have an emotional need to do that." A tool positioned primarily around emotional processing will not resonate — it must be positioned around practical outcomes (evidence quality, reducing retelling, supporting reviews).
- **Systemic change angle will resonate.** Wendy's deepest motivation is systemic. If BellaAssist can be framed as contributing to system improvement — not just individual convenience — that will land.
- **Mental health sensitivity is essential.** Wendy is acutely aware that mental health is still stigmatised. Any product messaging must treat mental health disability with the same gravity and legitimacy as physical disability.
