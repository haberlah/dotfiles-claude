## 2. Observable Behaviours & Decision Patterns

### Decision-making style
- **Pragmatic and action-oriented.** Prioritises "what needs to be done right now" — the brand name "Next Move" reflects this philosophy literally. Asks new participants: "What are the things you need done now in your life?"
- **Evidence-based but intuition-trusting.** Relies on structured processes but also trusts instinct built from prior SIL experience: "I have a very good grasp on what is needed to keep that funding."
- **Wait-and-see on regulatory change.** Refuses to react prematurely to NDIS policy shifts: "I stress about things when I need to. I don't need to stress about this at the moment." Did not rush registration when others spent $15K prematurely.

### Handling ambiguity
- Comfortable with uncertainty but manages it through over-preparation on evidence. After a rejected change-of-situation early in career, shifted to requesting "a million pieces of evidence just to cover that basis."
- Candid about gaps in knowledge: openly says "I have no idea" (on reforms, S1), "No" (on reasonable and necessary test, S5). Does not bluff.

### Prioritisation
- Highly structured weekly regime: Mondays dedicated to reviewing all participant plan portals, invoices, and funding. Weekend prep for Monday tasks. Allocates specific days for specific task types.
- Begins plan reviews 2-3 months before plan expiry. Targets submission 1-2 months before plan end.
- Ruthlessly prioritises efficiency: "I don't waste time. I don't like wasting time."

### Collaboration
- Invests heavily upfront in provider relationships — does one-on-one catch-ups, meets directors personally, reviews sample FCAs before referring. Sends detailed guidance on required language and wording at first engagement.
- Builds trust through radical transparency: sends invoices to families voluntarily, offers no cancellation period, copies all parties on documentation.
- Adapts communication style to participant/family preference — calls, texts, emails — and revisits preferences over time.

### Risk management
- Vets every provider before referral: "I'm so picky about who I refer... I will have to meet with the director first."
- Reports always frame around "worst day" scenarios and risk of funding withdrawal, not positive outcomes.
- De-identifies all data before using AI tools: "I always put John Doe."

### Adoption decision criteria
1. **Efficiency gain must be immediate and obvious.** Bailey adopted Astalty because onboarding went from minutes of manual work to "two minutes — press a button and it populates a profile."
2. **Per-user pricing, not per-participant.** Strong preference: "I probably wouldn't use a program that was per participant because as a sole trader, it makes more sense for me to do per user." Notes per-participant pricing would deter both sole traders and large companies.
3. **Low cost threshold.** Willing to pay $40-50/month for BellaAssist — benchmarked against Astalty at $75/month but adjusted down because it is "not a full customer service-based system."
4. **Must not duplicate existing functionality.** Strongly against budget tracking in BellaAssist because plan management portals already handle this. "That would be a waste of time for support coordinators."
5. **Must not require participant action.** The biggest adoption barrier: "The participant needing to be a part of it drains me already... nine times out of ten they will not do it."
6. **Security and de-identification are table stakes.** Currently uses external AI with manual de-identification. Would prefer a secure in-tool solution.
7. **Must integrate into existing workflow, not replace it.** Sees BellaAssist as a document hub and evidence tool sitting alongside Astalty, not replacing it. Expressed interest in it becoming a full CRM eventually.

### Technology attitudes
- Early adopter with strong practical filter. Enthusiastic about AI but sceptical of AI applications that replace human interaction (e.g., AI welfare checks, AI phone reception). Draws a clear line: "We're meant to be people-focused."
- Frustrated by other SCs' resistance to technology: "I think a lot of people are a bit more old when it comes to these things and I think it's detrimental."
- Values "What's New" feature notifications — appreciates when tools communicate their updates clearly.
- Prefers minimalist, simple UI: "I'm a big minimalist. I like simplicity."

## 12. Brand & Positioning Sensitivity

### Language and framing that resonates
- **"Streamline" and "efficiency"** — consistently responds positively to these terms across all sessions.
- **"Supporting the support coordinator"** — the framing of a tool that helps SCs help participants. Bailey explicitly validates this: "It really actually supports the support coordinator."
- **"Document hub"** — how Bailey naturally frames BellaAssist's core value to them: "I feel like that'd be a document hub for me."
- **Practical time savings quantified** — responds to concrete claims like "saves you from billing 1-2 hours to read a report."
- **Independence as participant value** — the single word Bailey consistently uses for participant-facing value.
- **"Fresh companies" and "up-and-coming"** — identifies with being new and modern; sees BellaAssist as a tool for companies that want to avoid "old systems."

### Values in professional tools
- Transparency (open invoicing, data portability, no lock-in)
- Simplicity (minimalist UI, clear language, not "trying to be anything crazy")
- Respect for SC judgment (tools that surface information but leave decisions to the SC)
- Speed (immediate efficiency gains, fast onboarding)
- Security (Australian-hosted data, PII redaction)

### Marketing red flags and turn-offs
- **Over-emphasis on participant self-service.** "If you focus too much on the participant aspect" it would put Bailey off. Most participants do not want to engage.
- **Per-participant pricing.** Would deter both sole traders and enterprises.
- **Duplicating existing tools.** Budget tracking or CRM features that duplicate Astalty or plan management portals are seen as "a waste of time."
- **AI replacing human connection.** AI phone reception, AI welfare checks — anything that removes the person-focused element: "That is a huge way to turn people off straight away."
- **Panic-inducing language.** Warned against "at risk of losing access to funded supports" messaging for plan expiry because funding typically rolls over.
- **Overly complex NDIS jargon directed at participants.** Recommends simplifying "submission" and similar terms for families.
- **$499/year participant billing.** Concerned about marketing intangible software to "generally low-economic families" who prefer tangible needs. Strongly advocates SC/enterprise billing instead.

### Positioning that would win Bailey's recommendation
- Frame as a professional efficiency tool for SCs, not a participant self-management platform
- Lead with document analysis and evidence management as the hero feature
- Price per user (SC), not per participant
- Position as complementary to existing CRM (Astalty) rather than competing with it
- Enterprise agreements for larger companies; affordable sole-trader tier
- Emphasise security credentials for AI document processing (resolves the manual de-identification workaround)
