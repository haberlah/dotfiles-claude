# The Evidence-Driven Advocate

## Summary

A methodical, evidence-building SC whose systematic practice is fuelled by deep conviction that the NDIS system fails participants. Constructs legislation-anchored evidence packages, gatekeeps allied health report quality through persistent correction cycles, and confronts planners directly when evidence is dismissed — escalating to tribunals, ombudsmen, and blunt conversations about participant safety when necessary. Approaches new tools through a trust lens: "Will this protect my participants and strengthen my evidence?" Takes pride in the craft of documentation. May be initially resistant to AI but can be converted through demonstrated reliability and ontological bounding.

---

## Behavioural Profile

### Decision-making
- **Participant safety overrides everything.** Amanda: "I'm not going to use funding on a stupid report for a client who needs support." Jane: works without a signed service agreement if the participant needs help now.
- **Evidence-first, legislation-anchored.** Amanda builds arguments from legislation upward, citing UN rights and NDIS Act provisions. Jane creates page-and-paragraph correction spreadsheets for allied health reports.
- **Confrontational when warranted.** Amanda: "They try to push back on me and I push back just as hard." Jane told a planner: "The decisions that you are making right now are potentially going to lead to someone taking their own life."
- **Avoidance of system traps.** Jane refuses change-of-circumstances requests because "99.9% of the time your evidence is ignored" and funding gets cut. Amanda won't sacrifice participant funding for her own SC report.

### Workflow patterns
- **Care team orchestration.** Amanda initiates care team meetings 3 months before plan end. Jane runs regular care team meetings with support workers and allied health.
- **Report quality gatekeeping.** Amanda returns "every single report" that doesn't meet standard. Jane creates elaborate correction spreadsheets. Kiersten uses ChatGPT to generate justifications and cross-checks against legislation.
- **Crisis-first triage.** Jane maintains a rolling 4-page to-do list; front page is urgent only. Kiersten carries the emotional weight of crisis calls that displace planned work.

### Technology stance
- **Trust is the adoption gateway.** Jane: "My thing is the trust... If I'm going to use it, I need to trust it." Amanda uses AI daily but with obsessive de-identification.
- **Craft pride in documentation.** Jane: "I find enormous joy in crafting letters and documents myself and I feel like I've put my heart and soul into it." The tool must augment this craft, not replace it.
- **Conversion is possible.** Jane shifted from "I don't like AI" to "persuaded" during co-design. The specific unlock was ontological bounding — knowing the AI stays within legislative parameters. Kiersten already uses ChatGPT pragmatically but benchmarks BellaAssist against it.
- **Privacy is non-negotiable.** Amanda manually strips all identifying information before any AI use. Lesley won't copy-paste AI output due to NDIS AI detection risk.

### Emotional register
- **High emotional investment.** Amanda has "sleepless nights" over three-month funding periods. Jane cried when breaking plan news to a chronically suicidal client. Kiersten carries clients' stories home.
- **Purpose-driven identity.** Jane: "I'd rather sit with someone chronically suicidal and navigate that rather than have to make sure people's bread's cut to the right size." This is not a job — it's a vocation.
- **Active burnout management.** Jane takes weeks off and communicates boundaries openly. Kiersten describes the emotional load as "the most unpleasant surprise." Amanda evolved from 40 participants to 15 complex.

## Motivations & Goals

### Functional Goals
- **Build** defensible, legislation-aligned evidence packages that withstand NDIA scrutiny and protect participant funding
- **Ensure** allied health report quality through persistent gatekeeping and correction
- **Protect** participants from systemic re-traumatisation (retelling stories, provider churn, evidence dismissal)
- **Coordinate** multi-disciplinary care teams to produce consistent, unified submissions
- **Reduce** administrative burden to create space for participant-facing work and personal wellbeing

### Emotional Goals
- **Feel** "sleep at night confidence" that everything possible has been done for each participant (Jane's phrase)
- **Feel** that the system acknowledges the stakes — that planners understand people's lives depend on these decisions
- **Feel** professionally respected, not dismissed as unqualified
- **Feel** that participant data and privacy are absolutely protected
- **Feel** that the craft and care invested in documentation is valued, not automated away

## Pain Points & Challenges

1. **[NDIA evidence dismissal — CRITICAL]** --> Comprehensive reports rejected without rationale --> Months of multi-professional effort wasted; emotional devastation when breaking news to vulnerable participants. Amanda: "200-page reports rejected as 'not reasonable and necessary' without explanation." Jane: escalated to ART.

2. **[Insufficient plan funding — CRITICAL]** --> Every plan is underfunded; trend worsening --> Participants run out of money mid-plan. Amanda: "a person could be dead" during the 3-month review cycle. Jane: PRC funding reduced from ~100 to 20 hours/year.

3. **[Unbillable work — systemic]** --> Service-driven ethos means working for free --> Amanda: "$20,000+ in free services" for a single complex participant. Jane: 15-18 hours/week on admin. Kiersten: billing ~50% of hours worked.

4. **[Report quality variance]** --> Allied health providers produce inadequate reports --> Amanda returns every report. Jane: 70% success rate on correction requests, rest require management escalation. Hours lost to rework.

5. **[Deficit language paradox]** --> Must use deficit-based language because showing progress triggers cuts --> Compromises therapeutic relationship. Jane warns clients about the language in their reports. System rewards appearing worse, not getting better.

6. **[Three-month funding periods]** --> Catastrophically mismatched to complex/degenerative cases --> Amanda: quarterly funding drops from $6,819 to $1,874. Jane: 5/5 pain, no clinical justification. Kiersten: 5/5 pain, monthly budget firefighting.

## Brand & Positioning Implications

| Dimension | Implication |
|---|---|
| **Messaging tone** | Empathetic, evidence-grounded, system-aware. Acknowledge the system is broken — don't promise to fix it. Lead with confidence and protection, not efficiency. |
| **Value proposition** | "Sleep at night confidence." A safety net and evidence shield. Position as augmenting their craft, not replacing it. |
| **Pricing** | Flexible — Amanda accepts $50-100/participant for complex cases OR business subscription. Jane open to participant-plan funding. Value must clearly map to hours saved on evidence work. |
| **Feature emphasis** | Document intelligence, inconsistency detection, evidence quality scoring, legislative alignment checking. NOT budget tracking or workflow management. |
| **Onboarding** | Incremental trust-building. Must allow verification of outputs. Jane endorsed step-by-step verification protocol. Show the ontological bounding (legislative parameters). |
| **Red flags** | AI-forward messaging, replacement narratives, "we'll write your reports for you," copy-paste output without verification, casual treatment of participant data, any suggestion the tool knows better than the SC. |
| **Channel** | Peer referral (strongest for this archetype), co-design and trial programs, SC community events, trusted colleague recommendations. |
| **Trust building** | Demonstrate accuracy first, then speed. Show the evidence behind the assessment. Allow them to verify and correct. The conversion arc is: sceptic --> verifier --> trusted tool --> advocate. Jane's journey during co-design is the template. |
