# Caroline Novinc -- Behavioural Profile Extract

## 1. Personal Profile & NDIS Context

### Caroline (Informant/Carer)
- Works for Air Services Australia.
- Originally from Canberra; lived away for approximately 25 years (Northern NSW / Byron Bay area).
- Currently living at her mother's house in Canberra after being made homeless following separation from her ex-partner Mark.
- Mother of four boys. Eldest son lives with her; three youngest (including Oscar, 14) live with their father Mark in the family home.
- Describes her ex-partner as "a narcissist" who was "coercively controlling" and abusive, particularly towards Oscar.
- Has experienced family violence, women's refuge, ongoing property settlement disputes, financial cut-off, and housing instability.
- Manages NDIS matters for both Bob and Oscar, despite significant personal stressors.

### Bob (NDIS Participant 1)
- Diagnosed approximately 3.5-4 years ago with Multiple System Atrophy (MSA) -- a progressive, fatal neurological condition.
- Was referred to a specialist in Sydney who confirmed the diagnosis. "It was a quiet ride home with my mate and I when I discovered what I've got."
- Has been on the NDIS for approximately 3.5-4 years, navigating multiple plans.
- Plan-managed through Nexus (Caroline initially said "agency managed" but was corrected by the interviewer; chose plan management to reduce administrative burden on Bob).
- Current support coordinator: Amanda (approximately 6 months). Previous SC: Community Options (described as doing "a pretty shit job" and "not running smoothly").
- Support workers provided by Dax, described as "hit and miss" from the beginning.
- Currently attending a Supported Independent Living (SIL) facility during the day (8 am to 8 pm) due to insufficient funding for in-home one-on-one support. Sleepover care at home.
- Allied health team includes: OT (Christine, described positively), physiotherapist (Luke, disrupted by SIL placement), speech pathologist, community nurse, psychologist (Daniel, recently engaged for telephone counselling).
- Bob's condition is progressive and degenerative: declining core strength, cognitive decline, reduced motor skills, difficulty using mobile phone, leaning to one side, falling forward while eating, reduced personal hygiene (showered once in a week at time of S2).

### Oscar (NDIS Participant 2)
- 14 years old. Diagnosed with ADHD, ADD, and ASD.
- Caroline disputes the diagnosis, believing Oscar's presentations are trauma responses from living in an abusive household. "I don't believe that he's got autism. I think he's suffering from trauma responses from living in an abusive household."
- On NDIS since approximately age 12 (around 2 years). Plan primarily covers behavioural support.
- Previous support coordinator described as "hopeless" -- gave Caroline a list of 15 providers with no direction or follow-up.
- Behavioural therapist (Jasmine Collins) saw Oscar only 5 times in 12 months, billed primarily for reports rather than therapy, and when Caroline tried to disengage her, the therapist told Caroline to "jam it" and questioned her parental authority.
- NDIS complaint about the therapist was dismissed with a standard letter.
- Caroline has no visibility into Oscar's current plan status, reviews, or budget.
- Describes Oscar as "a really bright kid... very smart, and he's funny" but with a "rough start to life" -- school refusal, attachment issues, aggression towards Caroline (punching, spitting).

## 2. Daily Life & Support Needs

### Bob's Daily Life
- Transported to SIL at 8 am, brought home at 8 pm. Sleepover carer at his home overnight.
- At the SIL: "He's basically been driven to the SIL and he sat in front of a TV for 12 hours and then they drive him home."
- Very small world -- "the only time he steps out of his house is when he goes to a doctor's appointment when he has to. Other than that... he just, yeah, he doesn't leave the house."
- Declining personal care: "He's only showered once this week. Like his, the level of care that he's getting is like, he looks like a homeless person. He hasn't shaved. He hasn't had a haircut."
- Equipment gaps: Wheelie walker delivered 3.5 years after diagnosis (2 weeks before S1), but unusable because Bob lacks hand strength for brakes. Sarah Steady delivered but does not fit around his recliner chair. Still waiting for appropriate lift chair. Shower rails only recently installed.
- Caroline and her sister were doing Bob's housework on weekend visits because support workers would just "sit there and watch TV for a couple of hours together and then leave" -- Bob would not ask them to do chores.

### Caroline's Carer Role
- Primary informal advocate and administrator for Bob's NDIS matters.
- Tries to attend all meetings with allied health professionals and support staff.
- Manages Oscar's NDIS matters without any support or communication from providers.
- Simultaneously dealing with separation, property settlement, housing instability, four children, and supporting Bob.

### Gaps Identified
- Insufficient funding for in-home one-on-one support, forcing SIL placement contrary to plan goals.
- Assistive technology delivered years late and often inappropriate.
- No proactive monitoring of either Bob's or Oscar's care.
- Physiotherapy disrupted by SIL placement.
- Oscar receiving effectively no therapeutic intervention despite having funded plan.

## 3. Decision-Making & Agency

### Who Makes Decisions
- Caroline is the de facto decision-maker for Bob's NDIS matters. Bob is present but minimally engaged -- "Bob probably doesn't do too much paperwork."
- Bob's denial about his condition and cultural reluctance to ask for help significantly limits his self-advocacy: "He's, and I still feel like he's in denial about his condition... a man would never ask another man to do his laundry or vacuum the floor."
- Bob is stoic and will tell professionals "everything's fine" even when his situation is deteriorating.
- For Oscar, Caroline has been the sole decision-maker but is now functionally excluded by distance, the ex-partner's interference, and providers who do not communicate with her.
- Caroline relies heavily on Amanda (SC) for guidance on NDIS processes. "If something changes... I'd ring Amanda."
- Her sister also helps with Bob's care and attendance at meetings when Caroline cannot be there.

### Sense of Control
- Caroline feels significantly disempowered by the system: "I just feel that a lot of things have sort of gone off the rails."
- Reports being "forced" into SIL arrangement for Bob due to funding insufficiency.
- Feels decisions about Bob's plan are made without adequate consideration of his changing needs (rollovers without review).
- With Oscar, feels "completely erased from his life" -- no communication from providers, no involvement in plan reviews.

## 5. Pain Points & Frustrations

### Assistive Technology Delays
- **Frustration:** Essential equipment arriving years late or being inappropriate.
- **Trigger:** Walker took 3.5 years. Sarah Steady does not fit the furniture. No appropriate lift chair yet.
- **Consequence:** Bob's condition has deteriorated beyond the point where early-delivered equipment would have helped. "He would have got used to even using one" if it had arrived earlier. Now he cannot use the walker because he lacks hand strength for brakes.

### Funding Insufficiency and Plan Rollovers
- **Frustration:** Plans rolled over without considering deterioration; change-of-circumstance request left with a "delegate" for months with no response.
- **Trigger:** NDIS rejected increased funding request by simply rolling over the plan without reviewing updated allied health reports.
- **Consequence:** Bob forced into SIL during the day, contrary to his plan goal of independent living. Financial anxiety: "Just worrying if I'm going to have enough money next month to pay for the bills."

### Unaccountable Providers
- **Frustration:** Behavioural therapist billing for reports rather than providing therapy (Oscar); support workers sitting idle (Bob).
- **Trigger:** Therapist saw Oscar 5 times in 12 months; support workers would not do housework because Bob would not ask.
- **Consequence:** Oscar's needs unmet. Caroline's complaint dismissed with a standard letter. For Bob, Caroline and her sister ended up doing the housework themselves.

### Administrative Burden with No Purpose
- **Frustration:** Support workers filling in dietary, medication, and sleep forms that nobody reviews.
- **Trigger:** Required paperwork sits in a folder on the kitchen bench unchecked.
- **Consequence:** "What's the point of it all?... I can't see the point of doing it if it's not being used to assist in any way."

### Support Coordination Failures (Pre-Amanda)
- **Frustration:** Community Options was "not running smoothly"; Oscar's SC gave a list of 15 providers with no direction.
- **Trigger:** Large organisations with too many clients; no proactive follow-up.
- **Consequence:** Wasted years for Bob. For Oscar, Caroline left to "sort it out myself."

### System Opacity and Navigation Difficulty
- **Frustration:** Multiple roles (plan manager, plan coordinator, SC) with unclear responsibilities.
- **Trigger:** Early days of Bob's NDIS journey -- "I wasn't really sure who to contact for certain things."
- **Consequence:** Confusion, repeated phone calls with no answers, questions going unanswered.

### Complaint Process Failure
- **Frustration:** NDIS complaint process arduous and ineffective.
- **Trigger:** Complaint about Oscar's behavioural therapist.
- **Consequence:** Complaint "went through several different channels" before receiving a dismissive response: "Oh, sorry, there's nothing we can do about it."

### Being Excluded as a Parent (Oscar)

- **Trigger:** Oscar living with father; therapist questioning Caroline's authority.
- **Consequence:** "It's like I have been completely erased from his life." No visibility into plan reviews, progress, or current status.

## 10. Value System & Priorities

### What Matters Most
1. **Practical outcomes over process** -- Caroline measures the system's worth by whether recommended supports actually materialise, not by how many reports are written.
2. **Independence and dignity for Bob** -- Remaining in his own home is the core goal. Being "forced" into SIL is the antithesis of what the plan promised.
3. **Accountability and follow-through** -- Providers should do what they are paid to do. Five therapy sessions in 12 months is unacceptable; AT recommendations that go unfulfilled for years are unacceptable.
4. **Being heard and included** -- As a carer and as a mother, Caroline wants to be part of decisions and kept informed. Being excluded from Oscar's care is deeply distressing.
5. **Efficiency of spending** -- Concerned that budget is "wasted on administrative stuff" and report-writing rather than direct service delivery. Wants to understand whether there are more cost-effective providers.
6. **Simplicity and clarity** -- Wants to know who to contact, what steps to follow, what is outstanding. The opacity of the system is itself a source of distress.

### Trade-offs She Would Make
- Chose plan management over self-management to reduce Bob's burden, accepting the cost.
- Would disengage a provider even at the cost of conflict if they are not delivering value (as she did with Oscar's therapist).
- Would consider an app/tool if it consolidated information and reduced the need to "dig" for documents.

## 12. Accessibility Considerations

### For Bob (Participant)
- **Motor impairment:** Declining fine motor skills; difficulty using a mobile phone. Any tool would need to be operable with minimal dexterity or via a carer/nominee.
- **Cognitive decline:** Increasing difficulty processing information. Easy Read feature specifically flagged as relevant. Complex interfaces would be inaccessible.
- **No computer access:** Phone-only user, and even that is becoming difficult. Large text, simple navigation, and carer/delegate access would be essential.
- **Stoicism/denial as a barrier:** Bob underreports his needs. Any self-assessment features need to account for the possibility that the participant will minimise their situation. Carer/nominee input pathways are critical.
- **Cultural considerations:** Bob's cultural background makes him reluctant to ask for help, particularly from male support workers for domestic tasks.

### For Oscar (Participant)
- **Child/adolescent:** Would require parent/carer-facing interface. Oscar himself is described as articulate and intelligent but would not likely engage with an administrative tool.
- **Living with non-cooperative parent:** Any tool would need to accommodate split-family situations where both parents may have different levels of access and involvement.

### For Caroline (Carer/User)
- **Digital literacy:** Moderate. Comfortable with email and video calls but self-identifies as not highly tech-savvy ("Luddites"). A phone app would work but she prefers a computer screen for detailed content.
- **Time-poor and stressed:** Interface must be efficient. She is managing two NDIS participants, a separation, housing instability, and employment. Anything that adds burden rather than reducing it will be abandoned.
- **Information overload:** Already overwhelmed by scattered documents, multiple provider contacts, and unclear processes. Consolidation and clarity are the primary needs.
