## 5. Pain Points & Challenges

### 5.1 NDIA Evidence Dismissal (Pain: Critical)
**Frustration**: Planners do not read submitted evidence, including allied health reports that cost thousands of dollars to produce.
**Trigger**: Planner proposed 10 hours/week support for client needing 6-8 hours daily; could not identify which evidence informed decisions; said "if you've got a problem, go for review."
**Consequence**: Jane cried when breaking plan news to chronically suicidal client. Escalated to ART, Commission, and Ombudsman. Now refuses to do change-of-circumstances requests.
> "every support coordinator you speak to would say that the biggest stressor in our work is the NDIA" [S1]

### 5.2 Insufficient Funding Allocation (Pain: Critical)
**Frustration**: Funding does not cover the work required, particularly for PRC clients.
**Trigger**: PRC funding reduced from historic ~100 hours/year to as low as 20 hours/year; SC funding similarly inadequate.
**Consequence**: Works for free regularly; 4-page rolling to-do list of urgent items; unable to complete reporting on time.
> "the most unpleasant surprise for new support coordinators [is] how little funding we have allocated to do the job properly" [S1]

### 5.3 Registration Burden vs Value (Pain: High)
**Frustration**: Audit compliance for registered providers is costly and stressful, with disproportionate benefit.
**Trigger**: Only gain from registration is ability to work with 3 NDIA-managed clients; rumours all SCs will need registration.
**Consequence**: Questioning financial viability of maintaining registration; facing another multi-thousand dollar outlay.

### 5.4 Deficit-Language Paradox (Pain: High)
**Frustration**: Must use deficit-based language in all reporting because showing any progress triggers funding cuts.
**Trigger**: NDIA interprets evidence of improvement as justification for reducing support.
**Consequence**: Cannot honestly represent client progress; must warn clients about language in their reports; therapeutic relationship compromised. [S3]

### 5.5 Administrative Overload (Pain: High)
**Frustration**: 15-18 hours/week on admin and reporting, with admin days routinely consumed by face-to-face visits and care team meetings.
**Trigger**: Rolling to-do list grows because crisis work takes priority; has reports "due months ago."
**Consequence**: Admin never catches up; blocks out dedicated admin days that "inevitably get filled up with face-to-face visits." [S1]

### 5.6 Rewriting Allied Health Reports (Pain: 5/5, Quarterly)
**Frustration**: Compiling page-and-paragraph correction spreadsheets for clinicians is extremely time-consuming.
**Trigger**: Reports contain inaccuracies or miss gold-standard content (e.g., missing HUD scores, no environmental assessment).
**Consequence**: Elaborate spreadsheet with citations and copy-paste text blocks; 70% success rate; some require escalation to management. [S1, S2]

### 5.7 Three-Month Funding Periods (Pain: 5/5)
**Frustration**: New three-month funding periods do not account for mental health conditions.
**Trigger**: Policy shift to shorter funding periods.
**Consequence**: Dramatically increased administrative burden with no clinical justification. [S1]

### 5.8 Emotional Toll of the Work (Pain: Ongoing)
**Frustration**: Regularly fields calls from distressed parents of clients who have attempted suicide; must remind them she is not a crisis service.
**Trigger**: Clients in crisis, family members seeking immediate help.
**Consequence**: Periodic burnout risk; took a week off to recover; acknowledges "the toll this work takes on a person." [S1]

## 12. Brand & Positioning Sensitivity

### What resonates with Jane
- **Humility and co-design authenticity**: Responded positively to BellaAssist team's stated approach of not "starting from a position that we know exactly what the problem is." Values genuine community input over government-style "co-design" theatre. [Optional]
- **Person-centred technology framing**: Engaged strongly with the concept of using technology to "centre the person again" who is "lost in a sea of specialisms." [Optional]
- **Team warmth and genuineness**: "Everyone seems really lovely... a really lovely genuine group of people." Views team culture as signal of product integrity. [Optional]
- **Participant benefit over SC cost**: Reacted positively to billing model where tool is funded through participant plans rather than as a cost to SCs. [S3]

### What would alienate Jane
- **Overclaiming or tech-first messaging**: Self-describes as AI-reluctant; would distrust anything that presents AI as a replacement for professional judgement
- **Removal of authorial control**: Report writing is a source of professional pride and identity. Positioning that suggests the tool "writes reports for you" would trigger resistance
- **Generic AI branding**: Specifically valued the ontological approach that limits AI to relevant knowledge. Generic chatbot positioning would undermine trust
- **Efficiency-only framing**: Time saved matters, but she would not redirect it to more client work. Messaging must acknowledge wellbeing and sustainability, not just productivity
- **Ignoring system brokenness**: Deep scepticism about whether better reports matter when planners do not read evidence. Any positioning that implies the tool fixes the system (rather than helping SCs navigate it) would ring hollow

### Positioning sweet spot
BellaAssist as a **safety net and evidence shield** -- gives the SC confidence they have done everything possible, provides defensible documentation trail, and shifts some of the burden from individual SCs to structured, legislatively-grounded technology. The "sleep at night confidence" frame that emerged in S2 is Jane's own language and likely the highest-resonance positioning for this archetype.
