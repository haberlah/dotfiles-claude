# Tom A — Behavioural Profile Extract

## 1. Personal Profile & NDIS Context

**Disability (participant on scheme):** James — diagnosed with global developmental delay (initial), subsequently ADHD and Delayed Language Disability (DLD). Functional impairment across speech, emotional regulation, and school performance. Paediatrician identified GDD around age 3-4 (approx. 2022). Later assessments confirmed ADHD and DLD through psychometric testing (Spencer Health, Jan-Feb 2025) and functional capacity assessment (Take Charge OT).

**NDIS history:**
- James placed on NDIS waiting list by mother (Amy), accepted November 2022.
- Initial plan likely NDIS-managed, later transitioned to self-managed at Tom's push (mid-2024).
- Successful plan review in 2024 — funding extended on the strength of the Take Charge FCA and paediatrician reporting.
- Funding cancelled February 2026 — ADHD alone insufficient; DLD did not meet functional impact severity threshold.
- Tom currently exploring review/appeal pathway.

**Plan type:** Began as plan-managed, transitioned to self-managed (approximately July 2024).

**Support structure:**
- Tom is the non-custodial father, residing in Victoria. James lives in New South Wales with mother (Amy), who is primary carer and was original NDIS nominee.
- No support coordinator was ever funded or engaged. The Local Area Coordinator / Early Childhood Coordinator (Denali) was the sole NDIA contact.
- Tom's partner Emma is mentioned but plays no direct role in NDIS administration.
- Tom is an ex-serviceman with 80 veteran's allowance points, exploring the education scheme and other veteran-linked supports for James following NDIS denial.

**Family dynamics note:** Significant ongoing conflict with James's mother (Amy) who is characterised by Tom as actively obstructive — cancelling appointments, refusing to agree to add Tom to the plan, providing assessment responses inconsistent with all other informants, and failing to provide a parental impact statement for the reassessment. Court orders were obtained in 2024 but the NDIS still required Amy's consent for plan changes, unlike schools and hospitals which accepted the orders readily.

## 5. Pain Points & Frustrations

### 5.1 Parental Consent Barrier
**Frustration:** NDIS required mother's consent to add Tom to active management, even with court orders in place.
**Trigger:** Court orders accepted by schools, hospitals, and other institutions — but not the NDIS.
**Consequence:** 12-24 months of being "benched"; delayed access to supports; inability to influence James's care despite legal authority.

### 5.2 NDIS Communication Opacity
**Frustration:** Cannot understand why James was denied — told he "doesn't meet the threshold" without specific explanation.
**Trigger:** Denial of funding in February 2026 after Tom assembled comprehensive evidence.
**Consequence:** Uncertainty about next steps; does not know what was missing or what would change the outcome; "clearer communication would be the biggest one" (S1).

### 5.3 LAC Responsiveness
**Frustration:** Contact with Denali (LAC/ECEI coordinator) was "hit and miss."
**Trigger:** Calls not answered, voicemails left, sometimes six or more contacts in a month before a response.
**Consequence:** Tom reverts to a "shotgun call" approach when something changes — calling repeatedly and hoping someone picks up.

### 5.4 Plan Opaqueness & Portal Usability
**Frustration:** "I still don't understand it" — the NDIS plan categories, budgets, and spending rules remain unclear.
**Trigger:** Being told to "download the app and go figure it out"; navigating the portal is "a rabbit hole."
**Consequence:** Tom stopped using the NDIS portal entirely; keeps documents in a physical desk folder; paid out-of-pocket costs he may not have needed to because he could not navigate reimbursement pathways. During S2 he actively compared the BellaAssist prototype to the NDIS portal in real time: "I'm looking at the NDIS, I'm like, it's just a bunch of numbers... not very user friendly" (S2).

### 5.5 Notification & Recognition Failures
**Frustration:** Despite compiling all reassessment documentation, the denial notification went to Amy, not Tom.
**Trigger:** Systemic default to the nominee/primary carer despite Tom's active role.
**Consequence:** Learned about the denial second-hand; felt "surprised and even a little bit peeved."

### 5.6 Financial Burden of Assessments
**Frustration:** Paid $2,700-$3,000 out-of-pocket for psychometric assessment; reimbursement would have gone to Amy.
**Trigger:** Assessment not initially supported under James's funding; self-managed plan reimbursement channels default to nominee.
**Consequence:** Financial loss; reinforced sense of systemic unfairness for the non-custodial parent.

### 5.7 Provider Reporting Gaps
**Frustration:** Paediatrician's report did not adequately support James's case — lacked NDIS-specific language and functional impact detail.
**Trigger:** OT and speech pathologist reports were strong, but the paediatrician "didn't really say anything" in NDIS terms.
**Consequence:** Tom believes this was a key factor in funding denial; "could you not have just used a tool like this and your true assessment?" (S2).

## 11. Brand & Marketing Sensitivity

**What would resonate:**
- Practical, outcome-oriented messaging ("this is what you need to do to bridge the gap")
- Emphasis on clarity and transparency — the anti-"rabbit hole"
- Visual simplicity — Tom noticed and valued colour coding and intuitive graph design in the prototype immediately
- Credibility through evidence alignment: the tool mapping against NDIS legislation and professional body standards resonated strongly
- Framing the tool as empowering the user to be a better advocate, not replacing professional judgment
- The paramedic metaphor: instant snapshot of current state plus accessible history

**What would alienate:**
- Overpromising ("we'll get your funding approved") — Tom himself flagged the "double-edged sword" of the approval prediction tool and would distrust claims that sound too good
- Jargon-heavy marketing that replicates the NDIS communication problem the tool is supposed to solve
- Deficit-framing of parents/carers — Tom is highly capable and has been navigating the system himself; messaging that implies helplessness would not land
- Any suggestion the tool replaces the need for quality professional reporting — Tom's frustration is that the paediatrician's report was inadequate, and he sees the tool as something that should help professionals as well as parents

**Price sensitivity:** Moderate. Tom paid $2,700-$3,000 out-of-pocket for an assessment. He stated he would "probably pay for" the Writing Assist feature. He is not price-averse when he perceives genuine value, but cost is a real concern post-NDIS-denial when privately funding supports.

**Trust factors:** Tom expressed comfort with the tool but wanted to de-identify documents before uploading during testing. This is careful, not resistant — he readily agreed to upload real documents and was enthusiastic about testing. The CEO's former role as Google's head of privacy was mentioned in S1 and appeared to land well.
