## 2. Observable Behaviours & Decision Patterns

### Work Organisation
- **Batches and bundles** similar tasks: geographic clustering of client visits (e.g., "Hornsby, Hornsby, Hornsby, Thornley — six people in one day"), and task batching ("give me 12 change of circumstances and I'll do them all in one day").
- **Monthly billing cycle**: processes ~100 clients on the 1st of each month; takes the best part of a day (~6 hours). Resists accountant's advice to bill weekly because setup overhead is similar regardless of frequency.
- Self-describes as "not a strong admin person" — prefers focused blocks over scattered admin.

### Client Intake & Relationship Building
- **Insists on physical meet-and-greet** before any service agreement: "I don't really actually start without a meet and greet." Absorbs this as a non-billable business cost.
- Emphasises independence and sole focus on SC when differentiating from competitors.
- Seeks "quick wins" early to build confidence and trust.

### Evidence & Submission Strategy
- Prioritises a single high-quality functional capacity assessment (FCA) as the "core document" — refers almost exclusively to Verve OT for these.
- Deliberately submits concise document packages (~half a dozen documents) rather than voluminous attachments: "I've seen like 64 attachments. It's a f****** waste of time."
- Focuses on **risk framing**: "Don't tell me someone needs therapy every two weeks. They will die if they don't."
- Actively refers to NDIS Act and legislation in planning meetings as an advocacy lever.
- Withholding strategy: prepares all review documents but does not submit unless NDIA requests them — avoids triggering unwanted plan scrutiny for families already satisfied with rolled-over plans.

### Team Management
- Builds competence through practical exposure: takes Kirsten to complex planning meetings so she can observe, then gradually transfers responsibility.
- Philosophy: "Do what I say, not what I do" (acknowledges his own admin weaknesses while coaching disciplined habits).
- Encourages autonomy: when Kirsten asked for help with a home-and-living report, told her to open the document first and assess what she actually needed help with — she never came back.

### Decision Heuristics
- Declines plans with very low hours (12–18 hours) as a signal to the NDIA that inadequate funding is unacceptable. Recognises others take them out of desperation.
- Routes non-complex referrals directly to Kirsten/Carl to build their caseloads; retains complex cases personally.
- Forward-plans housing/SIL conversations early ("planting the seed"), even when families are initially resistant.

## 10. Technology & Tool Evaluation

### Current Tool Stack
- **CRM**: SPLOSE — used for client files, billing, calendar. ~$350/year per user.
- **Email**: Primary communication channel.
- **SC report on business letterhead**: Custom concise report replacing the NDIS template (which "no one reads").
- **Work plan**: Informal; the mandated "support plan" for audit is considered "biggest piece of s***."
- Aware of **A-STALTY** (competitor CRM by Jonathan Power / Empowered Community Services) — better billing features but ~double the cost per user.

### Tool Evaluation Mindset
- **Pragmatic sceptic**: Open to new tools but acutely cost-conscious given fixed NDIS rates. "As a business, do we want to take on more costs? Not at the moment."
- **Burden test is paramount**: Any tool must pass the "does it create more work?" threshold. This was his dominant concern throughout S4 MVP testing.
- **Sees broader applicability**: Noted BellaAssist's tool could serve OTs, health districts, even the NDIA — not just SCs. "OTs could take it on board... they can look kind of silly when an AI tool scrutinises it and says you've been to uni for four years."
- **Time-saved would go to business, not clients**: "I'd probably spend more time on the business. Like I want to implement tools. I've for a month we've done our tax return. I haven't reviewed and signed it off."

### MVP Testing Feedback (S4)
| Feature | Reaction |
|---|---|
| Document processing / extraction | Significant improvement noted vs. prior test; "the guts of the tool" |
| Star rating system | Confusing — official NDIS documents rated "poor"; subjectivity problematic |

### High-Value Feature Requests (from Craig)
1. **Inconsistency check**: Cross-document contradiction detection — "I cannot read reports that are 70 or 100 pages long."
2. **Participant voice → NDIS-defensible blurbs**: AI converts subjective participant statements into evidentiary language.
3. **Higher intensity skill descriptor pre-check**: Tool assesses whether evidence meets criteria for higher intensity funding before submission.
4. **Copy-paste name/disability detection**: Flag when previous clients' details are left in current documents.
5. **Communication package for providers**: Rather than a list of errors, generate a suggested email to send to the allied health professional.
6. **In-and-out list screening**: Pre-check recommended supports against the NDIA's published list of funded/unfunded items.
7. **Proportionality / balance check**: Flag when care funding is very high but capacity building or SC funding is disproportionately low.

## 12. Brand & Positioning Sensitivity

### How Craig Evaluates Professional Services/Tools
- **Does it make me look competent?** The generated report's value was partly in perception: "Someone else might look at my work and go, like, what's this guy do? Like nothing."
- **Does it respect my time and expertise?** Rejects tools or features that create busywork. Objects to star ratings on NDIA documents because "that's their legislation."
- **Does it solve for the constraint I actually have?** Hours are limited per participant; the tool must be efficient within that constraint, not add review cycles.
- **Is the framing appropriate?** Pushed back on "Action Items and Next Steps" — reframed to "Proposals" because he already knows what to do.

### Positioning Implications for BellaAssist
- Craig would be sceptical of any positioning that implies SCs need the tool to do their job. He would respond well to positioning that frames the tool as:
  - An efficiency multiplier for already-competent SCs
  - A quality assurance layer for allied health documents (OT/behaviour support)
  - A way to elevate perceived value of SC work without adding hours
  - A training/confidence tool for less experienced team members (Kirsten use case)
- Price sensitivity is high given fixed NDIS rates — any tool cost competes directly with contractor wages.
- Would likely be an advocate/champion if the tool genuinely reduced burden and produced "copy-paste ready" outputs.

### Risk Factors for Engagement
- Will disengage if the tool surfaces noise (false positives, contextual errors, irrelevant flags).
- Will resist if the tool positions itself as replacing SC judgement rather than augmenting it.
- Regulatory uncertainty about SC's future creates existential cost-benefit anxiety about any new tool investment.

**Confidence: SUPPORTED** — Derived from stated preferences and observed reactions during MVP testing; no direct brand/positioning questions were asked.
